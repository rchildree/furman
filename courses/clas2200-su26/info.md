## Description

This course reads the three great epics of Greco-Roman antiquity — Homer's *Iliad* and *Odyssey* and Virgil's *Aeneid* — from beginning to end, in English translation. We will ask what these poems meant to the audiences who first heard them, how a poem composed for performance differs from one composed for readers, and why stories about a ten-year war and a ten-year commute have kept a hold on the imagination for nearly three thousand years.

No knowledge of Greek, Latin, or the ancient world is assumed. What is assumed is that you will do the reading — this is a discussion-heavy course, and it only works if everyone arrives having met the day's text.

## Background

CLAS 2200 satisfies the humanities core requirement and counts toward the Classics major and minor. There are no prerequisites. It pairs naturally with CLAS 2210 (Greek Tragedy) and CLAS 3300 (The Age of Augustus), but neither is required.

## Goals

By the end of this course, you will be able to:

- Read a long poem closely and talk about it precisely — scene by scene, line by line when it counts.
- Explain how oral composition shaped the Homeric poems, and how Virgil transformed an inherited tradition into something deliberately literary.
- Trace a theme (hospitality, rage, homecoming, empire) across all three epics and articulate how each poem reframes it.
- Write persuasively about literature using evidence from the text.

## Expectations

- Do the reading before class, with the text in hand (paper or tablet — but have it open).
- Participate. Quality matters more than volume, but silence for weeks at a stretch is not an option.
- Budget realistically: summer pace means roughly 60–80 pages of reading per class meeting.

## Materials

- Homer, *The Iliad*, trans. Emily Wilson (Norton, 2023)
- Homer, *The Odyssey*, trans. Emily Wilson (Norton, 2018)
- Virgil, *The Aeneid*, trans. Shadi Bartsch (Random House, 2021)

Other translations are acceptable if you already own them, but page references and passage identifications on exams will use the editions above.
